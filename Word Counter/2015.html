    Markets Lose Ground On an Up-and-Down Day

    By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSJAN. 13, 2015

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Stocks swung from gains to losses and almost back again.

    Wall Street opened Tuesday higher, encouraged by news on hiring and small-business confidence. Then it swooned in the afternoon as oil prices fell once more. The pendulum swung back late in the day as oil gained in after-hours trading, and stocks ended with small losses.

    From peak to trough, the Dow Jones industrial average swung 425 points.

    Stocks are having a jumpy start to the year as investors assess the effects of the collapse in the price of oil. The outlook for global growth also remains fuzzy as the recovery continues in the United States but economies in Europe and Asia struggle.

    Even though market volatility has picked up at the start of the year, investors should stay calm, said Janet Dougherty, a global investment specialist in Chicago at JPMorgan Private Bank. “You have to remember that we’ve been through an extended period where there wasn’t a lot of volatility in the equity markets, and now we’re just getting back to normalized levels,” she said.

    The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index fell 5.23 points, or 0.3 percent, to 2,023.03. The Dow slipped 27.16 points, or 0.2 percent, to 17,613.68. The Nasdaq fell 3.21 points, or less than 0.1 percent, to 4,661.50.

    Oil fell after the energy minister for the United Arab Emirates said on Tuesday that OPEC had no plans to curb production. Oil has slumped almost 60 percent since June.

    “At a certain point oil has got to find a bottom,” said Jeffrey Carbone of the wealth manager Cornerstone. “But for that to happen, somebody is going to have to flinch and cut production.”

    The market’s initial rise came after a survey suggested that the rapid pace of hiring in 2014 would continue.

    Employers advertised the most job openings in nearly 14 years in November, the Labor Department said. That suggests businesses are confident that strong growth will create more demand for goods and services.

    Job openings rose 2.9 percent to 4.97 million in November, the most since January 2001. More job vacancies generally lead to more hiring.

    A separate survey on small business showed confidence rising to an eight-year high in December.

    Plunging oil prices and stronger growth in the United States are expected to help propel global growth in 2015. But the World Bank, in the first of its twice-yearly Global Economic Prospects reports, nonetheless downgraded its forecast for global growth.

    Citing stagnation in Europe and a slowdown in China, the bank’s economists now estimate that the world economy will expand by 3 percent this year, down from a forecast of 3.4 percent in June. The bank expects the United States economy to grow 3.2 percent this year, up from 2.4 percent in 2014.

    Among individual stocks, McGraw Hill Financial, the owner of the Standard & Poor’s bond rating company, was the biggest gainer, rising $5.13, or 6 percent, to $90.89. The gains followed reports that the company was close to reaching a $1 billion settlement with the government on accusations it misled investors about its ratings of bonds backed by subprime mortgages.

    In government bond trading, the yield on the 10-year Treasury note was unchanged at 1.91 percent.

    The euro fell 0.5 percent to $1.1775 and the dollar slipped 0.2 percent to 117.89 Japanese yen.

    In metals, prices were mixed. Gold rose $1.60, or 0.1 percent, to $1,234.40 an ounce. Silver gained 59 cents, or 3.6 percent, to $17.15 an ounce. Copper fell 8 cents, or 3 percent, to $2.64 a pound.

    In other energy trading, wholesale gasoline fell 0.6 cent to $1.269 a gallon; heating oil fell 2.1 cents to $1.633 a gallon; and natural gas rose 14.8 cents to $2.943 per 1,000 cubic feet.
